How to ﬁnd your Niche - uncovering startups and research.

By Radhika Sethi

I'm a final-year student working at IISER in computational biology. My previous experiences include being part of the data science team at a startup, working as a research assistant at the University of California, Irvine, to name a few. Here, I'll discuss my experiences and how undergraduate students can gain valuable work experience in the startup and academic worlds. During my second year, I actively sought experience to understand what research genuinely meant, especially in the interdisciplinary fields. I also wanted to obtain work experience at a company and prepare for programming interviews for summer internships.

I discovered data science internship opportunities at various Bangalore-based startups through networking and the guidance of a mentor. I applied to many well-known startups and applied through a recommendation to one such Ed-Tech Startup, Shunya. Inc, and received a response from the team within a few weeks. I completed a take-home assignment and had a phone interview for it. Let's fast forward to when I joined the team. Shunya was working on developing a hyper-personalized learning platform. Our team worked on Data Science research for cognitive analysis, focusing on Item Response Theory, ontology, and knowledge graphs. We worked on statistical models for calculating maximum likelihood.

Networking on Linkedin and Twitter and browsing AngelList, are the most effective ways to find prospective startups. AngelList is a website that connects tech startups looking for seed investment with investors. These are early-stage companies that must develop and market their product in a fast-paced setting. Working for a startup might benefit you in two ways. The first is that you have more flexibility to build the product and idea from the bottom up. The second is that you deliver at pace, which means you must constantly expand your skill sets and develop excellent results while being mentored. Reaching out to early-stage founders and joining a startup is significantly easier than going for unicorns, which require a high profile and extensive work experience. (The premise is that working on a business at an early stage allows you to learn more). To summarise, working in a startup helps you grow your network and gain access to new opportunities while constantly honing your technical skills.

I also wanted to look into academic research and what it entails, bearing in mind what higher education in the field might involve. I was actively looking for research internships and summer/winter programs during my third year. I recall missing the Mitacs Internship deadline, which prompted me to take the process seriously and explore other options. International students are not eligible for several summer research programs offered by universities abroad; instead, I discovered summer school programs such as Quantum Computing Summer Schools- QuisKit and a summer school at University College London. I was awarded a scholarship to UCL's summer school, which piqued my interest in multidisciplinary research in computer science and biology. I got to apply computational methods to solve biological problems and spent time interacting with Ph.D. students and senior researchers in the field during summer school. I worked on tractography and neuroimaging. The summer school was when I discovered the numerous areas of computer science. I had the chance to work with clinicians/medics, computer scientists, mathematicians, and medical physicists who work with clinical collaborators. I realized that one doesn't need to be from a particular background to contribute to work in the field and that people from different backgrounds bring in various perspectives to work on problems.

After summer school, I realized my interest in the field and the various domains of biology. Genomic mutations (genetics) piqued my interest. On reading papers and following up on ongoing research, I reached out to a few professors. In a matter of days, I received responses from three professors, and after further discussion, I began working as a research assistant in bioinformatics at the University of California, Irvine. Because bioinformatics is an interdisciplinary field, I used my computational skills while also learning about the biology aspect of the problem. In the research group, I worked on neural variational inferences on TF networks. I used deep learning methods to understand the regulatory interactions between transcription factors and their target genes. I had the opportunity to lead an entire research project and contribute to another ongoing project at the time. Working at an international lab helped me build cross-cultural skills and understand how research labs in the USA work. We would have lab meetings twice a week to present our results and discuss questions related to the project. One learns teamwork skills and how to operate with different time zones, especially if it's remote work. My team and I reached out to various professors in June and July this year, as we were inclined to work on a thesis as part of our final year project. We put together a list of professors in India working in the domain, reached out, and connected with a few. We had the opportunity to discuss with them and learn about the ongoing research at their labs. After a few discussions, we realized our inclination to pursue our project at IISER in the domain of computational biology.

I've recently come across quite a few research opportunities in India. Microsoft Research, TCS Research, and a few labs across India actively look for research interns or fellows to contribute to their work and be mentored by a research scientist. It can be tough to get relevant industrial and academic experience as an undergrad student, but it is achievable. Find programs that accept overseas students or look for opportunities by attending conferences and networking with the attendees. Reach out to people in the field via LinkedIn and understand the process they took to reach there. It helps to understand the skill sets needed to get particular work experience. The best way to get research opportunities is by discussing with your professors and asking if they could recommend you to someone in the field. If not, reading papers and expressing your learnings and suggestions in an email to the professors you want to work with better demonstrates your interest to work under them rather than a generic email asking for a research position at their lab. Professors are more likely to reply if they see the student is interested in their work and has some potential ideas about it. At college, we also have research internships, initiated by Dr. Sunita Jahirabadkar, wherein students with similar interests as a professor, are matched to work on a research project, during summer break. Reach out to the professors you would like to work with and learn from, beforehand! I understand that it's not easy to ﬁnd your niche, but the ﬁrst step is always to identify what interests you. Maybe a workshop or a particular subject intrigued you. Identify the problems in the domain, potential problems that you would like to work on, and make a list. Search them up on Google and Google scholar, read papers on it (just the introduction/summary and the conclusion) and look at where the authors are, where they work, and their domain. You might ﬁnd some professors at college interested in the same ﬁelds too, reach out for a casual talk and understand their interests and point of view. Apart from that, corporate experience is necessary as well. Experiencing both helps to reason your interests and understand your goals. Think of each experience as an investment of your time and how you can build from those experiences. The most valuable thing I've learned throughout is that one needs to ﬁnd good mentors. Your growth is directly proportional to the kind of people you work with or who mentor you. Someone willing to spend time on you is way more important than chasing top opportunities where someone might not have enough time on their hands to take you through the initial work or understandings.


Best of luck! Feel free to reach out to me in case of questions, and try to pack in as much experience and explore as many domains as you can during your undergrad!

Cheers,
Radhika Sethi
